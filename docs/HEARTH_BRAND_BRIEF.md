# Hearth — Product & Brand Brief

> A single source of truth for what Hearth is, why it exists, how every
> feature is designed, and the logotherapy research each rests on. Written
> to be shared with a marketing or design AI to generate a logo and
> taglines. Last updated 2026-06-29.

---

## 1. What Hearth is, in one line

**Hearth is a daily home for finding meaning**, a quiet app that helps a
person notice meaning in the moments of an ordinary day and watch those
moments gather into a clear sense of what their life is about.

Tagline in use: **"Tend your why."**

---

## 2. Purpose, the problem it answers

Hearth is built on Viktor Frankl's **logotherapy**, the school of
psychotherapy founded on the premise that the primary human motivation is
not pleasure (Freud) or power (Adler) but the **will to meaning**, the
striving to find and fulfil meaning in one's life (Frankl, 1946; 1969).

Frankl named the central affliction of modern life the **existential
vacuum**: a pervasive sense of emptiness, boredom, and meaninglessness
that arises when a person has lost the instinctual and traditional
supports that once told them what to do, and has not found a meaning to
move toward (Frankl, 1946; 1978). Hearth exists to meet that vacuum, not
by prescribing a meaning, but by helping a person **discover their own**,
in the only place meaning is ever actually found: the concrete moment.

The guiding conviction, in Frankl's words: *"Those who have a 'why' to
live can bear with almost any 'how.'"* (Frankl, 1946, paraphrasing
Nietzsche.)

## 3. The intellectual foundation (logotherapy in brief)

Hearth's architecture and every feature derive from logotherapy's core
teachings. The marketing of Hearth should treat these as the bedrock.

### 3.1 The three pillars of logotherapy (Frankl, 1969)
1. **Freedom of will** — the human capacity to take a stance toward
   conditions, the last freedom that can never be taken away.
2. **Will to meaning** — meaning, not pleasure or power, is the deepest
   motivation.
3. **Meaning in life** — life holds meaning under all circumstances, even
   suffering.

### 3.2 The three avenues to meaning (the "meaning triangle") (Frankl, 1946; 1969)
Meaning is discovered in three ways:
1. **Creative values** — through what we *give to* the world: a work, a
   deed, a task only we can do.
2. **Experiential values** — through what we *receive from* the world:
   beauty, truth, nature, art, love, encounter with another person.
3. **Attitudinal values** — through the *stance we take* toward a
   suffering we cannot change. When we cannot change a situation, we are
   challenged to change ourselves.

**This triangle is the entire architecture of Hearth**, rendered in plain
language as **Give · Receive · Carry**.

### 3.3 Other core concepts Hearth uses
- **The meaning of the moment** — meaning is not abstract or global; each
  concrete situation poses a unique meaning waiting to be answered
  (Frankl, 1969; 1978).
- **Self-transcendence** — the human being is most fully themselves when
  reaching beyond themselves toward a meaning or another person; meaning
  is found outside the self, not by introspection alone (Frankl, 1966;
  1969).
- **Tragic optimism** — saying "yes" to life in spite of the "tragic
  triad" of pain, guilt, and death: turning suffering into achievement,
  guilt into change, and the transitoriness of life into responsible
  action (Frankl, 1984, "The Case for a Tragic Optimism"; Wong, 2009).
- **Dereflection** — relief from suffering by turning attention away from
  excessive self-observation toward meaning and others (Frankl, 1978).
- **Socratic dialogue** — drawing a person's own latent meaning into
  awareness through questions rather than advice (Frankl, 1969; Lukas,
  1984).
- **The defiant power of the human spirit** (*Trotzmacht des Geistes*) —
  the spirit's capacity to take a meaningful stance against fate
  (Frankl, 1946).

## 4. The architecture: Give · Receive · Carry

The three avenues are the navigation and the meaning of the whole app:

| Avenue | Plain name | Frankl's term | What it means |
|---|---|---|---|
| Creative | **Give** | creative values | meaning through what you offer: the deed only you can do |
| Experiential | **Receive** | experiential values | meaning through what you let in: beauty, awe, music, another person |
| Attitudinal | **Carry** | attitudinal values | meaning through how you hold what you cannot change |

The consoling logic the brand can lean on: **there is always a door to
meaning, even when one or two are closed.** When work collapses, love and
beauty remain; when everything is taken, the freedom to choose one's
stance remains (Frankl, 1946).

## 5. The features, and why each is designed that way

### 5.1 Today, the daily home
**What it is.** The home screen: a daily-changing quote presented as a
still, framed centerpiece; one daily invitation ("the meaning of this
moment"); and the meaning narrative in distilled form.

**Why.** It enacts **the meaning of the moment** (Frankl, 1969): rather
than asking "what is the meaning of life," it asks what *this* day, this
moment, is asking of you. The daily quote is a threshold, a pause before
meaning.

### 5.2 The meaning of this moment + the Meaning Log
**What it is.** A single daily prompt that rotates across the three
avenues (Give / Receive / Carry). The reader answers in a line and keeps
it. Kept lines accumulate into a Meaning Log.

**Why.** Logotherapy holds that meaning is **discovered moment to moment**
and is **unique to the person and the situation** (Frankl, 1969). The act
of noticing and keeping one true line per day is meaning-detection made
into a daily practice. The accumulating log embodies the principle that
the moments add up to a life, the empirical basis of **narrative
identity** (McAdams, 1993; McAdams & McLean, 2013).

### 5.3 Give
**What it is.** A daily "deed of the day," a small act of giving, plus a
short menu of ways to give (an act of kindness, a gratitude letter,
sharing what you know, giving your attention). Each is kept to the
Meaning Log.

**Why.** This is the avenue of **creative values** and the engine of
**self-transcendence**: meaning grows by reaching beyond oneself toward a
task or another person (Frankl, 1966; 1969). It is reinforced by the
strongest findings in positive psychology that giving lifts the giver:
acts of kindness (Lyubomirsky, Sheldon & Schkade, 2005), the gratitude
visit and letter (Seligman et al., 2005; Emmons & McCullough, 2003).

### 5.4 Receive (Attune + the Reading Room)
**What it is.** *Attune*: the reader describes how they feel, and Hearth
returns three songs and three poems chosen for the texture of the mood.
*The Reading Room*: a small, daily-curated set of essays, poems, and slow
news. Saved pieces go to the Nook.

**Why.** This is the avenue of **experiential values**, meaning received
through beauty, art, and encounter (Frankl, 1946; 1969). Two research
disciplines shape it:
- **The iso-principle** (Altshuler, 1948): meet the listener's present
  emotional state first, then let the music move them. Hearth matches the
  mood before it lifts it.
- **The sad-music paradox**: listening to sad music when sad is
  comforting, not deepening (Vuoskoski et al., 2012; Garrido & Schubert,
  2015), and music regulates mood in everyday life (Saarikallio &
  Erkkilä, 2007; North & Hargreaves, 2008). Reading fiction and poetry
  supports empathy and wellbeing (Mar et al., 2009; Billington, 2015;
  Croom, 2015). Savoring deepens experiential meaning (Bryant & Veroff,
  2007).

### 5.5 Carry (the meaning session + the companion)
**What it is.** When a feeling is heavy, the reader is met in a guided
session that moves through five movements: a seeing (being met where they
are), a widening (a Socratic question), **a companion** (a real or
literary figure who faced the same predicament or worse and still carried
light), a turning (toward meaning through one of the three avenues), and
a small step. Steadying practices (breathing, a shutdown ritual) sit
alongside.

**Why.** This is the avenue of **attitudinal values**, the stance taken
toward unavoidable suffering, the heart of logotherapy (Frankl, 1946;
1969). It uses logotherapy's signature techniques:
- **Socratic dialogue** to draw out the person's own meaning (Frankl,
  1969; Lukas, 1984).
- **Modification of attitudes** and **tragic optimism**, helping a person
  turn suffering into an achievement of the spirit (Frankl, 1984).
- **The companion / exemplar**: logotherapy works with meaningful models,
  figures who demonstrate that meaning can be found in the hardest places
  (Frankl drew on exemplars, including his own survival of the camps;
  applied logotherapy formalises the use of meaningful models, e.g.
  Lukas, 1984; Fabry, 1968). The companion is chosen for the *form* of
  the turning, never to rank or minimise the reader's suffering.

### 5.6 Your Meaning, this season (the Meaning Narrative)
**What it is.** A synthesis across everything the reader keeps (Meaning
Log, journal, saved pieces, sessions) into one short, evolving reflection
of how they make meaning, framed through give / receive / carry, with a
distilled glance on the home screen and the full prose one tap away. It is
provisional, time-stamped, and re-woven as the reader adds to it.

**Why.** This is the capstone. It rests on:
- **Narrative identity**: a person's sense of self is an internalised,
  evolving life story (McAdams, 1993; McAdams & McLean, 2013).
- **Provisional, revisable identity**: meaning and identity are tried on
  and revised, not fixed (Ibarra, 1999; "possible selves," Markus &
  Nurius, 1986).
- **Frankl's caution against typology**: meaning is unique to the person
  and discovered, never assigned by a category (Frankl, 1969). Hearth's
  narrative is therefore always "yours to recognise and revise, never a
  verdict," and never a personality type.
- **Measuring the presence of meaning**: the very tradition of measuring
  meaning began in logotherapy with the Purpose in Life Test (Crumbaugh &
  Maholick, 1964) and continues in the Meaning in Life Questionnaire's
  distinction between the *presence* of meaning and the *search* for it
  (Steger et al., 2006).

### 5.7 The Journal (Socratic prompts + a blank page)
**What it is.** A curated bench of evidence-based reflective prompts, the
ones closest to the reader's state surfaced first, plus a recent reflection
("Lately, in your journal") and an always-present blank page.

**Why.** The prompts enact **Socratic dialogue** (logotherapy) and draw on
the most replicated writing interventions (see references). The blank page
is **expressive writing** in its purest, most evidence-based form
(Pennebaker, 1997), and the honest catch-all for any feeling a prompt does
not hold.

### 5.8 The Nook
**What it is.** A private anthology of everything the reader has saved:
songs, poems, books, articles.

**Why.** A personal collection of one's **sources of meaning**, the
experiential-values avenue made into a keepsake; a record the meaning
narrative reads from.

### 5.9 Safety
**What it is.** When acute distress is detected, Hearth surfaces real
crisis resources alongside the session.

**Why.** Logotherapy is meaning-centred care, not crisis intervention.
Hearth is explicit everywhere that it is *a companion, not a clinician.*

## 6. Design principles (the spirit, for tone)

1. **The method is invisible; the experience is human.** The user never
   sees the words "logotherapy" or clinical language inside the product;
   they feel met, moved, and pointed somewhere that matters. (The
   intellectual lineage is named only where it builds trust, such as the
   landing page.)
2. **Self-transcendent, not self-absorbed.** Hearth turns a person
   outward, toward meaning and others, not inward toward endless
   self-monitoring (Frankl, 1966).
3. **Provisional and revisable.** Nothing about the person is fixed or
   typed; every reflection is theirs to revise (Frankl, 1969; Ibarra,
   1999).
4. **Evidence, not novelty.** Every prompt and recommendation is chosen
   for replication, not invention.
5. **Restraint.** Quiet, literary, warm without saccharine. No therapy
   speak, no hype, no gamification, no guilt.

## 7. Brand brief for logo and tagline generation

**Name.** *Hearth* — the fireplace at the heart of a home; the fire one
tends; warmth and a constant light to return to. The hearth is the oldest
human symbol of gathering, safety, and the tended flame. In Hearth the
fire *is* meaning: tended daily, it warms a whole life.

**Central metaphor.** **Light in darkness.** Frankl's image of the
"defiant power of the human spirit" is a small, steady flame that the dark
cannot put out. Secondary motifs: the tended fire, dawn, a threshold, a
home returned to.

**Essence in three words.** Meaning. Warmth. Light.

**The three avenues (for any tri-part logo or palette).**
- Give — warm, generative. Accent: Ecru / warm gold (#E1BE74).
- Receive — cool, open. Accent: Carolina Blue (#C9D8DA).
- Carry — soft, tender, grounded. Accent: Pale Dogwood (#EBCDC2).

**Core palette (in use).**
- Old Lace (paper): #F9F4E6
- Midnight Green (ink): #1F4045
- Warm gold / Ecru: #E1BE74
- Carolina Blue: #C9D8DA
- Pale Dogwood: #EBCDC2

**Typography (in use).** Serif headlines (Fraunces, italic for warmth);
quiet monospace for small labels; humanist sans for body. Editorial,
Aesop / Frama register.

**Voice.** Quiet, considered, warm, specific, literary. A thoughtful
friend who reads widely. Never clinical, never hype, never gamified.
**No em dashes** (use commas or periods).

**Positioning rule (important for taglines).** Describe only what Hearth
*is* and the value it brings. **Never** position by contrast ("unlike
other apps," "not a mood tracker"). Stated clearly, Hearth's uniqueness is
self-evident.

**Taglines in use / to build from.**
- "Tend your why." (primary)
- "A daily home for meaning."
- "Find the meaning in your days."
- "Give. Receive. Carry."
- "Meaning lives in the moment, and the moments add up to a life."

**What a logo could evoke.** A single tended flame; a hearth; a small
light; a threshold or doorway (the "door to meaning"); a triad for
give / receive / carry; dawn. Restrained, warm, literary, timeless. Avoid:
clinical/medical motifs, generic wellness leaves, brain icons, chat
bubbles, anything loud or gamified.

## 8. Design system: the principles behind every detail

The visual language is not decoration; it is the philosophy made visible.
Every choice serves one feeling: **a quiet home for meaning, warm without
saccharine, a constant light.** The register is editorial and restrained
(in the spirit of Aesop and Frama): it treats the reader as an intelligent
adult and lets a page rest.

### 8.1 The governing principle
**Restraint is the brand.** Generous whitespace, few elements, quiet
confidence. Nothing is loud, gamified, or infantilising. If an element is
not earning its place, it is removed. Warmth comes from typography, light,
and word choice, not from ornament.

### 8.2 Color
**A six-colour palette, no exceptions.**

| Role | Name | Hex |
|---|---|---|
| Paper / stage | Old Lace | `#F9F4E6` |
| Second paper / soft insets | Blush Isabelline | `#F2EAE8` |
| Ink / type / dark sections | Midnight Green | `#1F4045` |
| Feature accent, warm (Give) | Ecru / warm gold | `#E1BE74` |
| Feature accent, cool (Receive) | Carolina Blue | `#C9D8DA` |
| Feature accent, soft (Carry) | Pale Dogwood | `#EBCDC2` |

Supporting "deep" tints, used only for **readable text on light grounds**:
Ecru-deep `#A8893E`, Blue-deep `#5E8388`, Dogwood-deep `#9A6E5E`; lifted
inks Midnight Green-2 `#2D5258` and Green-3 `#486A6E` (the default body
colour).

**The colour law (load-bearing):**
- **Old Lace and Midnight Green carry every screen.** They are paper and
  ink. Everything works in just those two.
- **The four accents are feature accents only**, used as **full colour
  blocks that own a single section**. They are **never** used as
  gradients, tints, or chrome decoration. A section is either paper, or
  fully one accent, never a faint wash of one.
- **Never pure black or pure white.** Neutrals are tinted toward the
  brand hue; "white" is Old Lace.
- The three accents map to the three avenues: **Ecru = Give, Carolina
  Blue = Receive, Pale Dogwood = Carry.** This mapping is consistent
  everywhere (tab accents, the daily moment block, the avenue glance).

### 8.3 Typography
Three families, each with one job:
- **Serif: Fraunces** (fallbacks Iowan Old Style, Georgia). All headlines
  and quotes. Often **italic**, which is where the warmth lives. Light
  optical weights.
- **Sans: Inter** (system fallback). Body text and buttons.
- **Mono: JetBrains Mono.** Small all-caps labels, kickers, dates,
  attributions, metadata.

**Scale and weight** (hierarchy through size and weight contrast):
- Display headline: ~38px, weight 340, line-height ~1.02, tight tracking
  (`-0.02em`). Scales up as a hero (the daily quote reaches 38px+ on
  desktop).
- Title: ~26px, weight 360. Section: ~20px, weight 400.
- Body: 15px, weight 380, line-height 1.55, colour Green-3.
- Kicker / eyebrow: ~10.5px sans, weight 500, **uppercase**, letter-
  spacing `0.22em`.
- Mono label: ~9.5px, uppercase, letter-spacing `0.18–0.22em`, muted.

**Rules:** italic carries emotional weight; the second line of a headline
is often italic. Body line length is kept comfortable (max-width ~560–600px).
Quiet, considered, a sentence can rest.

### 8.4 Backgrounds and surfaces
- **Old Lace is the stage**, the default ground for nearly everything.
- **Blush Isabelline** is the soft inset, used for quiet cards and reflection
  blocks, a half-step warmer than the paper.
- **Full accent blocks** give a single section ownership (the daily moment
  block in its avenue colour; the landing's Give/Receive/Carry blocks).
- **Midnight Green** is reserved for dark sections and the closing call,
  ink becomes ground, type becomes Old Lace.
- **The hearth glow** is the one permitted gradient: a single, very soft
  radial warm light (Ecru at low opacity, fading to nothing) behind the
  home masthead, the "constant light" made visible. It is atmosphere, not
  a blob, and it is the only place a gradient appears.
- **White/lace bookends**: screens open and close on paper, with colour
  used as punctuation in between, a rhythm, not a wash.

### 8.5 Layout, spacing, and shape
- **Generous, varied whitespace.** Spacing is rhythmic, never uniform;
  identical padding everywhere reads as monotony.
- **Edge gutters** of ~22px on mobile; a centred content column capped at
  ~760px on larger screens so text never runs too wide.
- **Hairline rules** (1px at ~10–18% ink opacity) divide content; a
  diamond glyph (◆) marks a still moment.
- **Sharp corners.** Border-radius is `0` almost everywhere, editorial,
  printed, not soft-app rounded. (Pills are reserved for small mono tags
  and the save button.)

### 8.6 Components
- **Editorial rows over cards.** Lists are hairline-divided rows, not
  boxed cards. Cards are used sparingly and **never nested**.
- **The door row**: a large tappable row with a serif word, a one-line
  description, and a quiet arrow that drifts on hover. The primary
  navigation pattern.
- **The moment block**: a full accent-colour section with a kicker,
  a serif prompt, and an inset field, prompt and answer as one unit.
- **Buttons**: sharp, uppercase sans, generous tap area. Primary in Ecru
  ("ember") or Midnight Green; secondary as a hairline ghost.
- **Inputs**: borderless except a single underline; serif, 17px (so iOS
  never zooms on focus); italic placeholder.

### 8.7 Motion
- **Ease-out, exponential.** Transitions use curves like
  `cubic-bezier(.22, 1, .36, 1)` (ease-out-quint/expo). **No bounce, no
  elastic.**
- **Forwards-only fade-ins** on a small translate-Y; the steady state is
  always fully visible (so a skipped frame never hides content).
- **Never animate layout properties.** Motion is transform and opacity
  only (e.g., the door arrow drifts via `translateX`, content settles via
  a gentle fade).
- Page transitions are quiet (lift, slide, reveal, cross), 0.3–0.5s.

### 8.8 Iconography and imagery
- **Minimal line icons**, stroke ~1.3–1.5, round caps. The avenue icons
  are deliberately human: **an open hand (Give), a flower (Receive), a
  heart (Carry).**
- **The botanical and fire metaphors are retired from chrome.** No leaves,
  no sprigs, no flame icons in navigation. (The flower icon for Receive is
  the one sanctioned botanical mark; the product name is still Hearth and
  the wordmark sets in italic serif.)
- **Photography**, when used, is quiet and unhatched; placeholders are calm
  fields of accent colour, never busy.

### 8.9 Anti-patterns (never do these)
- **No em dashes** in any copy. Use commas, periods, or restructure.
- **No side-stripe accent borders** (a thick coloured `border-left` on
  cards or callouts). Use full borders, background tints, or nothing.
- **No gradient text, no glassmorphism, no drop-shadow-heavy "hero metric"
  cards, no identical icon-card grids.**
- **No gamification, no streaks-as-pressure, no badges, no emoji, no
  exclamation hype, no therapy-speak.** Continuity is shown as light that
  accumulates, never as guilt.

### 8.10 Accessibility and mobile
- Tinted neutrals and deep accent variants keep text legible on every
  ground; ink-on-paper contrast is the default.
- Inputs are 17px (no mobile zoom-on-focus); the bottom navigation respects
  the device safe-area; the layout guards against horizontal scroll.
- Type and the hero quote scale up responsively so the experience reads as
  a poster on a wide screen and a calm column on a phone.

### 8.11 The throughline
Paper and ink, a little warm light, three colours for three ways to
meaning, serif that can be tender, and a great deal of room to breathe.
Every detail says the same quiet thing: **this is a place to find meaning,
and you are welcome here.**
